A Framework for Intent-Driven
Navigation Design

A structured method for decomposing user intent into Jobs, Tasks, and Activities — then mapping each level to the right navigation paradigm.

Designed for product designers defining information architecture, developers building navigation systems, and product managers who need to explain and defend IA decisions. Self-contained — hand it to any of those audiences and it stands on its own.

The Process

Step 01DecomposeWhat are my users' Jobs to Be Done?

Users don't think in pages, menus, or modules. They think in outcomes they're trying to achieve. A navigation system should mirror that intent hierarchy — not your org chart, not your database schema, and not whatever your backend team decided to name the API endpoints.

This model builds on Jobs to Be Done theory (the Job level) and Hierarchical Task Analysis (the Task and Activity levels) — see the Appendix for origins and further reading. Most users have 3–7 Jobs — that's normal and expected. Your navigation system needs to accommodate all of them.

The Three Levels

Job
Change frequency: Rarely
The overarching outcome a user is trying to achieve. Emotionally resonant, context-independent, and product-independent. This is the why.
Changes when...
The user's role, goals, or life circumstances change. A promotion, a career shift, a new business model — these are the forces that redefine a Job. For platform administrators, the Job is the outcome they're enabling for others — "Enable my team to collaborate securely" — and it changes when organizational needs change, even though every Task underneath is product-specific.
Example
"Keep my team's financial health strong so we can grow sustainably" — true whether the user has your software, a spreadsheet, or a paper ledger.
Task
Change frequency: Periodically
A discrete, goal-oriented unit of work that advances the Job. Has a clear start and end, requires deliberate effort, and can be planned or delegated.
Changes when...
Workflows, processes, or business needs evolve. New tools get adopted, teams restructure, compliance requirements shift — the Job stays the same but the work to advance it changes.
Example
"Create and send monthly budget reports" might become "Set up automated reporting dashboards" — same Job, different Task.
Activity
Change frequency: Frequently
A concrete, atomic interaction within a Task. Happens in real-time, is tool-dependent, and represents the unit of UI interaction design.
Changes when...
The tool or interface is redesigned. A button becomes a drag target, a form becomes a wizard, a manual step gets automated. Same Task, different interaction.
Example
"Select date range, pull data, choose template, export PDF" — each of these would change if the reporting tool was redesigned.

The Litmus Tests

In practice, the boundaries between levels blur. These tests help you classify correctly:

Is it a Job?

Ask: "Would this still be true if the product didn't exist?" Jobs are product-independent. If the statement only makes sense inside your product, it's probably a Task.

Is it a Task?

Ask: "Would someone put this on a to-do list?" Tasks are plannable. If you can imagine delegating it to a colleague with a brief explanation, it's a Task. If you'd have to point at the screen, it's an Activity.

Is it an Activity?

Ask: "Does this require a specific UI element to exist?" Activities are tool-dependent. If the action would change significantly with a redesigned interface, it's an Activity.

Still not sure if it's a Task or an Activity?

Ask: "Would the user plan a session around doing this?" If the user would set aside time — "I need to spend 20 minutes doing my updates" or "I need to go through all the new feedback this morning" — that's a Task. The individual interactions within that session (clicking status dropdowns, reading each feedback entry) are Activities. Tasks get intentional time blocks. Activities happen within them.

Key Insight

Jobs rarely change — they're tied to the user's role and goals, not your product. Tasks change periodically as workflows and business needs evolve. Activities change frequently, every time the interface is updated. Your navigation at each level should match this rate of change. If your primary navigation is shifting every time you redesign a feature, that's a signal you've mapped Activities at the Job level.

Decomposing for Multiple User Types

Most products serve more than one persona. A project management tool serves Project Managers, Developers, and Designers — each with overlapping but distinct Job maps. Don't try to decompose all personas simultaneously. Instead:

Decompose one persona at a time. Give each persona its own complete Job → Task → Activity hierarchy. This keeps each map clean and prevents you from prematurely averaging distinct mental models into a single muddled structure.

Then overlay the maps. Once you have 2–3 individual maps, lay them side by side. Look for three things: shared Jobs (both the PM and the Developer care about "Keep the project on track"), shared Tasks under different Jobs (both check project status, but for different reasons), and persona-exclusive Jobs (only the Developer has "Ship high-quality code"). Mark these overlaps and divergences — they directly feed the role-based navigation decisions in Step 04.

Calculate your overlap ratio. If two personas share more than 70% of their Jobs, you're likely building one navigation with different defaults. If they share less than 30%, you may be building two different products. The space in between is where navigation design gets interesting — and where this framework earns its keep.

A Note on Cross-Job Tasks

As you decompose, you'll notice that some Tasks serve multiple Jobs. "Generate a financial report" might advance both "Keep stakeholders informed" and "Ensure regulatory compliance." Don't force these Tasks into a single Job during decomposition — acknowledge them and move on. We'll address how to handle cross-Job Tasks in the Map step.

Step 02MapHow does intent translate to navigation tiers?

You've decomposed your users' Jobs, Tasks, and Activities. Now you need to map them to navigation tiers. But before you assign levels to UI patterns, you need to answer a foundational question: what organizes your user's mental model?

The answer determines how your JTBD hierarchy translates to navigation structure. Get this wrong and your navigation will fight the user's thinking — even if every individual component is well-designed.

Choosing Your Navigation Anchor

Users organize their work in one of four dominant mental models. Each model produces a different mapping from Jobs, Tasks, and Activities to primary, secondary, and in-page navigation. Most products are hybrids, but one model dominates — identify that dominant model and let it drive your primary navigation.

Process-first
Jobs = Primary nav
The user thinks in distinct phases or workspaces, each with its own tools and mindset. They say: "I'm in research mode" or "I'm doing my weekly review." The phases are the primary organizer.
Diagnostic
Do your users describe their work as distinct modes or phases? Does each phase involve different tools and a different mindset? Are the phases relatively independent — the user works in one phase at a time, not tracking entities across phases?
Real-world examples
TurboTax — linear flow through Personal Info → Income → Deductions → Review → File. Shopify Admin — Products, Orders, Marketing, Analytics are distinct workspaces. HubSpot — Marketing, Sales, Service are separate hubs with their own toolsets.
PROCESS-FIRST MAPPING JOB / Phase ——→ Primary Navigation Always visible "Research" / "Delivery" Top bar, sidebar root | v TASK ——→ Secondary Navigation Within phase context "Run customer interviews" Tabs, sub-sections | v ACTIVITY ——→ In-Page Navigation Contextual / transient "Schedule interview call" Buttons, forms, inline actions
Entity-first
Entities = Primary nav
The user tracks specific things — features, accounts, projects, cases — that persist over time and move through a lifecycle. They say: "How's the Johnson account doing?" or "Where are we on Feature X?" The entity is the anchor; Jobs become lenses on that entity.
Diagnostic
Do your users refer to their work by name? Do they track specific entities that move through multiple Jobs over time? Is their primary question "what's the status of X?" rather than "what phase am I in?"
Real-world examples
Salesforce — Accounts, Contacts, Opportunities are the primary objects. Each has tabs for different concerns. Jira — Issues move through workflows; the issue is the anchor. GitHub — Repositories are entities with Code, Issues, PRs as Job-scoped tabs within each.
ENTITY-FIRST MAPPING ENTITY ——→ Primary Navigation Always visible "Feature X" / "Acme Acct" Entity list, entity detail | v JOB / Phase ——→ Secondary Navigation Tabs within entity "Discovery" / "Delivery" Phase tabs, concern tabs | v TASK → ACTIVITY ——→ In-Page Navigation Contextual / transient "Write requirements" Forms, editors, inline actions
Queue/Inbox-first
Incoming stream = Primary nav
The user's work arrives as a stream of items that need processing — tickets, messages, notifications, requests, approvals. The user doesn't go find work; work comes to them. The primary organizing construct is the queue or inbox, filtered and sorted by urgency, assignment, or status. The user's first question isn't "what phase am I in?" or "what's the status of X?" — it's "what should I work on next?"
Diagnostic
Does work arrive continuously rather than being initiated by the user? Is the user's primary interaction processing items from a list? Are the items transient (resolved and archived) rather than persistent entities tracked over long periods? Does the user's effectiveness depend on processing speed and prioritization?
Real-world examples
Zendesk — ticket queues filtered by status, priority, and assignment. The queue IS the navigation. Gmail — inbox with labels, filters, and priority sorting. Slack — message streams organized by channel and thread. PagerDuty — incident queue that narrows the UI to what needs attention now. GitHub Pull Requests — review queue for code that needs processing.
QUEUE/INBOX-FIRST MAPPING QUEUE VIEWS ——→ Primary Navigation Always visible "My Queue" / "Unassigned" Filtered lists, inbox views | v ITEM DETAIL ——→ Secondary Navigation Tabs within item "Conversation" / "History" Concern tabs, detail panels | v ACTIVITY ——→ In-Page Navigation Contextual / transient "Reply" / "Escalate" Buttons, quick actions, shortcuts
Reactive vs. Proactive Work

Queue/Inbox-first is the model for reactive workflows — where the system surfaces what needs attention. The other three models assume proactive workflows — where the user initiates navigation toward their goals. This distinction matters because reactive navigation needs to bring work to the user (prioritized queues, notification badges, urgency indicators), while proactive navigation needs to let the user find work (browsable hierarchies, search, hub pages). Many products blend both: a support tool is queue-first for agents but process-first for managers building reports.

Activity/Mode-first
Dominant activity = Separate mode
The user's core work requires deep, immersive focus — writing, designing, coding, editing video. When they're in the activity, everything else should disappear. The product has two worlds: an immersive creation mode and a management/browse mode, connected by an explicit mode-switch.
Diagnostic
Is there one dominant activity that requires full-screen focus? Does that activity account for the majority of usage time? Would showing navigation during this activity be a distraction rather than a help?
Real-world examples
Figma — File browser (management) ↔ Canvas (design). Two distinct modes with different nav. VS Code — The editor dominates; explorer, terminal, and panels are secondary. Substack/Ghost — Full-screen editor for writing, dashboard for everything else. Adobe Premiere — Edit, Color, Audio, Deliver are immersive activity modes.
ACTIVITY/MODE-FIRST MAPPING MANAGEMENT MODE ——→ Standard Navigation Top bar, sidebar "Browse projects, check Jobs and entities organized metrics, manage settings" using process or entity model ↑↓ explicit mode-switch IMMERSIVE MODE ——→ Minimal / Hidden Navigation Reduced or none "Write the article, Full-screen focus, contextual design the interface" toolbars only, nav recedes
Most products are hybrids

Pure instances of any single model are rare. Salesforce is entity-dominant with process elements (opportunity stages). Figma is activity-dominant with entity-secondary (file browser). A support tool might be queue-first for agents but entity-first for managers tracking specific accounts. Identify your dominant model, let it drive primary navigation, then layer the secondary models into lower tiers.

Mapping Principles (Apply to All Models)

Regardless of which model you chose, three principles govern how the JTBD levels translate to navigation tiers:

Persistence mirrors stability. Whatever occupies your primary navigation — whether that's Jobs (process-first), entities (entity-first), queue views (queue-first), or modes (activity-first) — should be the most stable element of the experience. It should rarely change between releases. Secondary and in-page navigation can evolve more freely.

Screen real estate mirrors scope. The primary anchor earns the most persistent screen space (header, sidebar root). Secondary concerns live inside that anchor's territory. In-page interactions live inside the page.

Interaction cost mirrors commitment. Switching between primary-level items is a significant context shift. Switching between secondary items should be lightweight. In-page activities should require near-zero navigation.

The Persistence Test

If a navigation element disappears when it shouldn't, or persists when it's irrelevant, you've got a level mismatch. Two legitimate exceptions: in activity/mode-first products, the immersive mode deliberately hides primary navigation — this is an explicit mode-switch, not a mismatch. In queue-first products, entering an item's detail view may collapse the queue to a narrow panel or hide it entirely — this is acceptable if the user can return to the queue in one interaction. The test still applies within each mode.

Handling Cross-Job Tasks: Job Gravity

In the Decompose step, you may have identified Tasks that serve multiple Jobs. Now you need to decide where those Tasks live in the navigation. Three approaches, each with different tradeoffs:

ApproachHow it worksBest when...Tradeoff
DuplicateThe Task appears under multiple Jobs as separate entries.The Jobs have genuinely different workflows around the same Task, and implementations are diverging.Users distrust the nav ("which is real?"). Maintenance doubles.
Single homeThe Task lives under one Job only. No cross-links.Usage from the secondary Job is negligible (<5%). The pointer overhead isn't worth it.Secondary-Job users can't find the Task without being told.
Job GravitySingle canonical home under the most-used Job. Lightweight pointers from other Jobs lead to the same destination.Multiple Jobs legitimately need the Task with significant usage from more than one.Pointer mechanisms add design and engineering work.

Job Gravity is the recommended default for most cross-Job Tasks. In entity-first products, Job Gravity is less of a concern because entity scoping naturally separates the same Task into different contexts. In queue-first products, cross-Job Tasks are rare because the queue itself is the organizing principle — items arrive pre-categorized.

Job Gravity in practice (process-first or hybrid): JOB: Stakeholder Reporting JOB: Compliance | | |-- Create monthly report <------------+ (pointer, not a copy) |-- Schedule distribution |-- Audit trail review +-- Track read receipts +-- File regulatory docs The report Task lives under "Stakeholder Reporting" because that's where 80% of usage originates. Compliance gets a pointer to the same destination.

The Four Pointer Mechanisms

When using Job Gravity, these mechanisms create paths to a Task's canonical home without duplicating it:

1. Cross-link in secondary nav. Under the secondary Job's section, include a "Related" grouping. Style it visually distinct from primary Tasks.

2. Contextual shortcut. When a user is working in the secondary Job and needs the Task, surface it inline.

3. Search / command palette result. The Task appears in ⌘K regardless of current Job context.

4. User-pinned favorite. The user pins the Task to their personal quick-access area.

Step 03ScopeWhat are my product's navigation constraints?

Before assessing behavioral complexity or selecting paradigms, take five minutes to profile your product across six dimensions. Your answers directly constrain which navigation patterns will work and which will break. Work through each question and note where your product falls.

1. How many top-level Jobs does your product support?
≤5 Jobs: You can likely expose all of them in a single navigation layer — top bar or bottom tabs. Start with flat paradigms and keep it simple.
5–15 Jobs: You need a nav pattern that handles volume — side navigation with groupings, or a hub with categorized entry points. Consider a command palette as an accelerator.
15+ Jobs: No single structural paradigm holds them all. You need a compound approach — hub-and-spoke plus search, or role-based filtering to reduce the visible set per user.
2. How deep is your Task tree?
1 level (Tasks directly under Jobs): Tabs or segmented controls within each Job. No breadcrumbs needed.
2 levels: Collapsible sidebar sections handle this well. Consider breadcrumbs as a supplement.
3+ levels: The sidebar handles the top two levels; breadcrumb drill-down handles the rest. Without breadcrumbs, users will get lost. Supported by NNG's enterprise complexity research.
3. How varied is your user expertise?
Uniform (most users at similar skill level): One navigation system serves all. Optimize for the dominant expertise level.
Moderate spread: Structural nav for everyone, command palette as an accelerator for power users. Role-aware defaults can help surface different starting points.
Extreme spread (novices to power users): You need layered navigation — visible structural nav for discovery, keyboard shortcuts and ⌘K for speed, possibly guided flows for onboarding. See Jared Spool on trained-use vs. walk-up-and-use interfaces.
4. How concentrated is usage?
Highly concentrated (~80% of sessions involve 2–3 Tasks): Elevate those Tasks to Job-level prominence. Everything else can be secondary. Optimize the critical path ruthlessly.
Evenly distributed: No single Task dominates. Navigation needs to give equal weight to many options — side nav or hub patterns work better than top bars with limited slots.
Mixed (some daily Tasks, some quarterly): The quarterly Tasks can't be buried — they need to be findable when needed, even if they're not prominent daily. Search and command palette become important safety nets.
5. How much navigation learning will users tolerate?
Near-zero: Navigation must be immediately obvious. Favor familiar patterns — top tabs, bottom bars, visible labels. No hidden interactions. Steve Krug's Don't Make Me Think is the classic reference.
Moderate: Users will invest some time if the product is central to their work. You can use richer patterns (collapsible sidebar, ⌘K) if you provide good onboarding.
High: Users will learn complex navigation because the product is mission-critical and used daily. Training budgets exist. You can optimize for power-user efficiency over first-use simplicity. See NNG's enterprise UX research.
6. What platforms does your product serve?
Desktop-only: You have full access to side navigation, top bars, hover states, keyboard shortcuts, and multi-panel layouts. Optimize for screen real estate and pointer precision.
Responsive web (desktop + mobile browser): Your composition formula needs a responsive strategy. Side nav typically collapses to a hamburger or bottom sheet. Tabs may move from top to bottom. Hover-dependent interactions (mega-menus, contextual tooltips) need touch alternatives. Plan how each of your four layers adapts.
Native mobile (iOS/Android): Platform conventions apply. iOS favors bottom tab bars; Android favors top tabs and navigation drawers. ⌘K becomes pull-down search. Contextual actions become swipe gestures or long-press menus. Your primary and secondary layers must respect platform-native patterns or risk feeling foreign.
Multi-platform (desktop + native mobile): You're maintaining two composition formulas that share a JTBD hierarchy but may use different paradigms at each layer. The Job and Task mapping stays identical; the paradigm selection diverges per platform. Document both.
What to do with your answers

Your scoping profile directly feeds the next two steps. High Job count + deep Task tree + extreme expertise spread + multi-platform = you're building an enterprise-grade layered navigation system. Low Job count + shallow Tasks + near-zero tolerance + mobile-only = you're building a consumer-style flat navigation. Most products land somewhere in between — your specific combination of answers will tell you which paradigms to prioritize in Step 05.

Step 04AssessHow do roles and context shape the navigation?

Three behavioral dimensions constrain your paradigm options: role-based flexing operates at the Job level (different roles have different Job maps), adaptive flexing operates at the Task and Activity levels (navigation changes based on what the user is doing right now), and user context shapes how every tier should feel (the same Task needs different navigation behavior depending on the user's state of mind).

Role-Based Navigation — Job-level flexing

The question: how different are the Job maps between your user roles, and therefore how different should the navigation be?

ApproachHow it worksWhen to useRisk
Separate appsEach role gets its own application with its own nav.Roles share <20% of Jobs. ServiceNow's admin portal vs. employee self-service — completely different Job maps.Maintenance doubles.
Role-filteredOne nav, items shown/hidden by role.Roles share core Jobs but differ in access.Ghost nav — users sense hidden items.
Role-aware defaultsSame nav, different starting points and pinned items by role.Same Jobs, different frequency patterns.Lowest risk. Full nav remains explorable.
Recommendation

Start with role-aware defaults. Only escalate to role-filtered when Jobs genuinely diverge. Only build separate apps when Job maps have less than 20% overlap. If you decomposed for multiple personas in Step 01, your overlap ratio tells you exactly which approach to use.

When roles overlap heavily

When roles share more than 70% of their Jobs, treat them as one role with different defaults rather than separate role buckets. A PM and a team lead with 90% Job overlap don't need different navigation — they need different starting points. The PM lands on a project dashboard; the team lead lands on the team view. Same nav, different defaults. Only invest in role-filtered or separate navigation when the Job maps genuinely diverge (<70% overlap), which usually means the roles serve fundamentally different audiences or functions within the organization.

Adaptive / Contextual Navigation — Task & Activity-level flexing

TypeJTBD LevelHow it worksExampleRisk
Workflow-stateActivityNav changes based on workflow progress.Editor toolbar shifts from editing to review tools when doc enters "Review" state."Where did my button go?"
Frequency-adaptiveTaskFrequently-used items gain prominence.Slack's sidebar reorders channels by recent activity.Spatial memory breaks.
Data-state-drivenTaskSystem data changes navigation priority.An overdue SLA ticket rises to the top of the queue. A blocked critical-path issue surfaces a warning banner.Users lose sense of control if overused.
Mode-switchingJob (border)Entire nav restructures on mode change.Figma: file browser → canvas. Different Jobs, different nav.Two mental models.
Data-State-Driven Priority

This is distinct from frequency-adaptive navigation. Frequency-adaptive responds to what the user does often. Data-state-driven responds to what the system's data says is urgent right now — SLA breaches, blocked dependencies, overdue tasks, anomalous metrics. The navigation priority isn't just about user behavior patterns; it's about the state of the work driving prominence. Use sparingly: if everything is urgent, nothing is. Reserve data-state escalation for genuinely time-sensitive or high-impact signals.

The Cardinal Rule

Never remove navigation options silently. If something will disappear, move, or change, the user needs to understand why. Adaptive navigation must be transparently adaptive.

User Contexts — How state of mind shapes navigation behavior

Most navigation frameworks ignore the user's state of mind. But the context a user brings to a Task — whether they're uncertain, expert, exploring, or under pressure — changes how the navigation tier should behave, even when the structural mapping stays the same.

How to identify which contexts apply to your users

Listen to language. In user interviews and support tickets, anxious users say "I'm worried about..." or "what happens if I...?" Confident users say "I just want to..." or "why can't I just...?" Exploratory users ask "what does this do?" or "what's this for?" Urgent users say "I need to fix this right now" or "this is broken."

Look at session patterns. Short sessions with high page-per-minute rates suggest urgency or confidence. Long sessions with low page-per-minute suggest exploration. Sessions that start and abandon midway suggest anxiety. Your analytics can surface these patterns without any qualitative research.

Map to user lifecycle. New users are more likely to be anxious or exploratory. Power users are more likely to be confident. Users returning after an incident are urgent. Your onboarding cohort vs. retained cohort vs. reactivated cohort roughly maps to these contexts.

Four user contexts matter most for navigation design:

Context: Anxiety / First-time uncertainty

What it feels like: Fear of making a mistake, uncertainty about consequences, worry about getting locked out or losing data.

Navigation implication: Narrow the navigation. Reduce choices. Guide with steppers and linear flows. The user doesn't want freedom to explore — they want to feel safe and guided.

Example: Stripe's onboardingA first-time merchant setting up payments. Stripe collapses the full dashboard into a focused checklist — a guided stepper that isolates the user from complexity.

Context: Confidence / High-frequency flow

What it feels like: Mastery, impatience with unnecessary steps, desire for speed. "I know exactly what I need — get out of my way."

Navigation implication: Minimize structural navigation. Provide keyboard shortcuts, command palette access, and direct-action buttons that bypass the hierarchy.

Example: Salesforce Global ActionsAn experienced sales rep logging their 15th call of the day. Salesforce's Global Actions "+" button lets them create a record from anywhere, bypassing hierarchy entirely.

Context: Exploration / Discovery

What it feels like: Uncertainty about what questions to ask, interest in browsing, building a mental model. "I don't know what I don't know."

Navigation implication: Expose multiple entry points with enough context to help self-selection. Hub layouts, visible tab bars, and card-based overviews work well.

Example: Google Analytics 4 HomeA new marketing manager opening analytics for the first time. GA4's home screen shows summary cards — each a window into a different dimension — helping the user self-select which rabbit hole to explore.

Context: Urgency / Crisis response

What it feels like: Pressure, time sensitivity, tunnel vision. "Something is broken and I need to fix it now."

Navigation implication: Strip away non-essential options. Surface critical Tasks in one click from anywhere. The interface should focus down to just what's needed.

Example: PagerDuty's Incident ViewAn IT admin during a production outage. PagerDuty narrows the entire UI to the active incident — timeline, responders, runbooks, status. Normal navigation recedes.
Connecting Context to Structure

The JTBD hierarchy tells you what tier of navigation something belongs to. The user's context tells you how that tier should behave. An anxious first-timer and a confident power user may be performing the same Task at the same tier — but the navigation should feel different for each. When selecting paradigms in the next step, test your choices against these four contexts. Ask: "Does this paradigm serve my anxious users? My confident users? My explorers? My urgent users?" If it fails any context that represents a significant portion of your user base, you need an additional layer or an adaptation strategy.

Parallel Views Are Tasks, Not Activities

Board view, List view, Timeline view, Calendar view — these are Task-level. Each view represents a different way of engaging with the same work: a Board emphasizes status and flow, a Timeline emphasizes scheduling. Use tabs or a segmented control, not a dropdown. Tabs expose all options simultaneously, which matters because users often switch between views as part of their workflow. A dropdown hides the options, adding friction to a high-frequency switch.

No structural hierarchy visible
Filter configuration complexity
Queue anxiety (overwhelming volume)
Hard to surface non-queue Tasks
ExamplesZendesk — ticket views as the primary workspace; filters, macros, and saved views customize the queue. Gmail — inbox with labels, stars, and smart categories. Linear — issue lists with custom views that function as the primary navigation. GitHub Pull Request review queue — filtered list of PRs awaiting review.
Anti-pattern: The Unfiltered FirehoseHundreds of items with no saved views, no default sorting, and no visual priority signals. The user has to configure the queue before they can use it. Provide sensible defaults: "My items," "High priority," "Recently updated."

Enterprise: The default pattern for support, operations, and approval workflows. Pair with saved views and keyboard shortcuts for power users. Consumer: Inbox patterns (email, notifications, messages) — sort by recency, group by sender/thread.

Composing Your Navigation System

Individual paradigms are building blocks. The real design decision is how you layer them. A well-composed navigation system has up to four layers, each serving a distinct purpose. Tension arises when two paradigms compete for the same layer, not when you have "too many" paradigms total.

Layer 1: Primary Structure

One paradigm that serves your navigation anchor — the model you identified in the Map step. This is the backbone of your navigation.

If your anchor is Jobs/phases (process-first) and you have ≤5: top nav bar. If 5–15: side nav. If 15+: hub & spoke.

If your anchor is entities (entity-first): side nav or list view for the entity collection. Hub & spoke if entities are highly distinct.

If your anchor is a queue (queue-first): work queue / filtered list as the primary content area, with a thin top bar or sidebar for queue view switching (My Queue, Unassigned, All).

If your anchor is modes (activity-first): the primary structure is the mode-switch mechanism itself. The management mode then gets its own primary structure using any of the above.

Layer 2: Secondary Structure

One paradigm that serves the tier inside your primary anchor. This is where most Task-level navigation happens.

Tab bar or segmented control when you have 3–5 parallel options within the primary anchor — Job-phase tabs inside an entity, Task tabs inside a Job workspace, detail tabs within a queue item. Collapsible sub-sections within a side nav when the secondary tier has more than 5 items or needs grouping. These two are mutually exclusive at the same level.

Layer 3: Depth Handling (conditional)

Only needed if your Task tree goes beyond 2 levels. Most consumer products skip this layer. Most enterprise products need it.

Breadcrumb + drill-down is the standard depth handler. It works alongside any primary and secondary structure — the side nav handles the top two levels, breadcrumbs handle everything below. If you don't need depth handling, skip this layer entirely. Adding breadcrumbs to a shallow product creates visual noise without utility.

Layer 4: Accelerators (additive)

These paradigms layer on top of any structural combination. They never conflict with structural paradigms because they operate in a different interaction mode — invoked on demand rather than persistently visible.

Command palette (⌘K) — for users who know what they want. Add when your Scope step revealed moderate-to-high expertise or mixed usage concentration. Contextual / floating actions — for Activity-level interactions. Add when core Tasks involve manipulation (editing, creating, configuring) rather than just consumption (reading, reviewing). Both accelerators can coexist. Neither counts toward structural complexity.

The Composition Formula

[Primary] + [Secondary] + [Depth] + [Accelerators]

One paradigm per structural slot. Two in the same slot = conflict. Empty primary or secondary = gap. Depth and accelerators are optional.

Composition Examples

Salesforce (entity-first):
Side nav (entities) + Tabs (concerns per entity) + Breadcrumbs + ⌘K

Instagram (process-first):
Bottom tab bar (Jobs) + Full-screen pages (Tasks) + None + Contextual actions

Figma (activity-first):
Mode-switch (browse ↔ canvas) + Side panel (layers/assets) + None + ⌘K + Contextual toolbar

Zendesk (queue-first):
Work queue (ticket views) + Tabs (ticket detail sections) + None + Macros + Keyboard shortcuts

Step 06AuditHow do I know my navigation works?

Seven principles for auditing your navigation system. Each includes conceptual framing, a decision question, and an implementation example — color-coded by audience.

Conceptual (Designers, PMs) — the why
Decision (Practitioners) — the what do I do
Implementation (Developers) — the how it looks

Principle 1: Frequency Overrides Hierarchy

Conceptual

If 80% of sessions involve the same Task, that Task earns Job-level prominence — regardless of logical position. Optimize for what users do, not what the IA says.

Decision

Question: Are your 3 highest-frequency Tasks reachable in ≤1 click from anywhere? Heuristic: >40% of sessions = persistent Job-level shortcut. Pre-launch, estimate frequency from user research, competitive analysis, and prototype testing — refine with real analytics post-launch.

Implementation

"Create Invoice" lives under Finance → Billing → Invoices, but it's the most-used Task. Give it a "+" button in the header, a ⌘K shortcut, and a pinned sidebar entry.

Principle 2: Stability Matches Persistence

Conceptual

Top nav should change as rarely as your Jobs — almost never. In-page controls can change every release.

Decision

Question: Has top-level nav changed in 6 months? Heuristic: Primary nav ≤1x/year. Secondary quarterly. In-page every sprint.

Implementation

GitHub's top nav (Code, Issues, PRs, Actions, Projects) has been stable for years. New features add within sections, not alongside them.

Principle 3: Context Must Be Preserved

Conceptual

Switching Tasks within a Job should preserve state. It should feel like switching tabs, not closing one app and opening another.

Decision

Question: Task A → Task B → Task A — is state preserved? If switching causes data loss or scroll reset, it's a hard transition when it should be soft.

Implementation

Notion: switching database views preserves filters, sort order, and scroll position. The lens changes; the context persists.

Principle 4: Progressive Disclosure, Not Confusion

Conceptual

Don't expose Activity-level nav until the user commits to a Task. Each tier reveals the next. But the user must always see which Job and Task they're inside. Disclosure forward; visibility all the way back.

Decision

Question: At the deepest point, can the user tell which Job and Task they're inside? If only a page title — add breadcrumbs or a sidebar path.

Implementation

Stripe: sidebar shows Job section, header shows Task entity, breadcrumbs show depth. All three tiers visible simultaneously.

Principle 5: Escape Velocity

Conceptual

From any point, a user should reach any higher level in ≤2 interactions.

Decision

Question: Clicks from deepest point to Job level? ≤2 = good. 3 = okay with breadcrumbs. 4+ = broken.

Implementation

Three escape mechanisms: persistent sidebar, un-truncated breadcrumbs, command palette. Most complex products need at least two.

Principle 6: Navigation as Wayfinding

Conceptual

At every moment, navigation must answer three questions. If any is unanswerable, the system is broken.

Decision

Walk 5 representative flows. At each screen, ask all three:

1. Where am I? → Active states, breadcrumbs, page titles 2. Where can I go? → Visible nav options, available actions 3. How do I get back? → Back buttons, breadcrumbs, persistent nav If any answer is unclear at any point, the navigation system is broken.
Implementation

"Where am I?" — Active sidebar highlighted, title matches label. "Where can I go?" — Sibling Tasks as tabs, Activities as toolbar buttons. "How do I get back?" — Clickable breadcrumb, persistent sidebar, working browser back.

Principle 7: Navigation Must Feel Instant

Conceptual

Structural correctness means nothing if navigation feels slow. Every transition in the navigation hierarchy — switching Jobs, opening Tasks, returning to a previous state — should feel like it happens in response to the user's action, not after it. In high-frequency tools used hundreds of times daily, navigation latency is the difference between a tool that helps and one that frustrates.

Decision

Question: Do your three highest-frequency navigation transitions complete in under 200ms perceived time? Heuristic: Primary nav switches should feel instant (<100ms). Secondary nav switches should feel fast (<300ms). Depth navigation (drill-down) can tolerate up to 500ms if a loading indicator appears within 100ms. If your most-used transition involves a full page reload or visible spinner, it's too slow — regardless of what the performance metrics say.

Implementation

Optimistic UI for tab switches (render immediately, backfill data). Preload adjacent views on hover or focus. Skeleton screens for deeper transitions. Linear and Notion both pre-cache sidebar items so switching between pages feels instant — the content appears before the URL finishes updating.

Final Heuristic

Map your Jobs and Tasks on paper first. Count the Jobs. Measure the Task depth. Note the frequency patterns. Identify your navigation anchor. Then pick paradigms and compose them using the formula. The hierarchy dictates the paradigm, never the reverse.

Using this framework: Follow the six steps in order. Decompose (1), Map (2), Scope (3), Assess (4), Select (5), Audit (6). Resist jumping to paradigm selection — the steps before it are what make the selection defensible.

ReferenceCommon Questions

Edge cases and ambiguities that come up when applying the framework to real products.

A Task looks the same in two different Jobs — do I need Job Gravity?

It depends on whether the user is asking the same question or a different question when they perform the action.

Same Task, multiple Jobs: "Generate a financial report" is the same work whether the user arrives from Stakeholder Reporting or Compliance. The output is the same, the workflow is the same — it just serves two purposes. This is a cross-Job Task. Job Gravity applies: pick a canonical home, add pointers from the other Job. Another example: "Export CSV" might serve both "Analyze my data" and "Share results with my team" — same export, different purpose. One home, pointers from the other.

Same Activity, different Task context: "Review analytics" during Discovery (understanding user needs) and "Review analytics" during Measurement (evaluating a shipped feature) involve the same screen but fundamentally different questions. The user needs different filters, different metrics, different framing. These are genuinely different Tasks that happen to share an Activity. No need for Job Gravity — design each Task's view appropriately for its context. In entity-first products, entity scoping resolves this naturally.

My product blends queue-first and entity-first — which wins?

This is common in products like CRMs and support tools. The key question is: does the user start their day by opening a specific entity, or by scanning a queue?

If agents start by working through a ticket queue and only open specific tickets from that queue, the queue is primary and the entity (ticket) is secondary. If account managers start by opening a specific account and then reviewing its associated tickets, the entity is primary and the queue is a secondary tool within it.

Many products legitimately need both: a queue-first view for processing work and an entity-first view for investigating specific items. In this case, treat them as two modes within your primary navigation — "Queue" and "Accounts" as peer items in the top bar or sidebar — similar to how activity-first products have management and immersive modes.

How do I handle navigation for AI-assisted or automated features?

As products embed AI suggestions, automated workflows, and proactive surfacing, a new navigation question emerges: where do system-initiated items live? AI-suggested next actions, automated alerts, and predictive surfacing are not user-initiated navigation — they're the system pushing items into the user's sightline.

Treat AI/automated items as a layer that sits alongside the structural navigation, not within it. A "Suggested" or "For You" section in a queue, an AI sidebar panel, or inline suggestions within a Task view are all valid patterns. The key principle: AI-surfaced items should never replace or reorder structural navigation without user consent. They're accelerators, like ⌘K — additive, not structural. The user must always be able to fall back to the JTBD-driven hierarchy they know.

AppendixOrigins & Further Reading

This framework draws from two intellectual traditions. Understanding their origins helps you adapt it to contexts not explicitly covered here.

Jobs to Be Done (The Job Level)

JTBD theory holds that users "hire" products to make progress. The Job is the fundamental unit — stable, emotionally resonant, product-independent. Three strands inform this framework:

Clayton Christensen popularized JTBD for innovation strategy. A milkshake competes with a banana and a bagel because the Job ("make my commute less boring") is product-independent.

Tony Ulwick (Outcome-Driven Innovation) systematized JTBD into a quantitative methodology — Jobs as measurable, prioritizable functional outcomes.

Bob Moesta (The Switch Framework) focuses on emotional dimensions — push, pull, anxiety, inertia. His work directly informs the user context section in Step 04.

Hierarchical Task Analysis (The Task & Activity Levels)

HTA (Annett & Duncan, 1967) decomposes work into goals, sub-goals, operations, and plans. Activity Theory (Kaptelinin & Nardi) extends this with attention to tools and social dynamics.
