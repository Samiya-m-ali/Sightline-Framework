Context

Why Does Navigation Keep Breaking?

Navigation is one of the hardest parts of product design to get right — and one of the easiest to get wrong incrementally. Teams invest real thought into their information architecture. The challenge is that navigation decisions compound: each feature launch, each new user role, each platform addition adds pressure to a structure that was built for a simpler version of the product.

Over time, patterns emerge. Users struggle to find features that exist because labels reflect internal team structures rather than user goals. Navigation changes with each release cycle because it's anchored to the feature roadmap rather than stable user outcomes. Onboarding takes longer than it should because new users have to reverse-engineer the product's internal logic. Support tickets accumulate around "how do I..." questions that the navigation should have answered on its own.

The underlying challenge isn't a lack of effort — it's a lack of shared structure. The work of connecting user intent to navigation decisions tends to happen informally: in someone's head, on a whiteboard that gets erased, in a design file that diverges from implementation. The thinking happens, but it doesn't persist across the team or survive the pressures of a shipping schedule.

AI-assisted navigation — copilots, conversational agents, intent-driven routing — is reshaping how users reach destinations, and its adoption is accelerating. But these tools route users through a navigation architecture; they don't remove the need for one. The AI needs the same semantic structure — what are the Jobs, what are the Tasks, which mental model dominates — to resolve intent correctly. Where AI changes the calculus is covered in What About AI?

Sightline gives that thinking a structured, repeatable home. It provides a shared language for connecting what users are trying to accomplish to how they navigate your product — so the architecture stays grounded in intent as the product evolves.

Context

Using This Guide

Sightline focuses on the orientation layer — keeping users grounded in where they are, where they can go, and how to get back. Knowing where it starts and stops will help you get the most out of it.

When to Reach for Sightline

Sightline works best after you've done foundational user research — you know who your users are, what they're trying to accomplish, and where current navigation is failing them — and before you start wireframing screens. It sits in the gap between "we understand our users" and "we're designing the interface." If you're working on a new product, it gives you your first navigation architecture. If you're working on an existing product, it gives you a structured way to evaluate and restructure what's already there.

What to Pair It With

Sightline focuses on the orientation layer — keeping users grounded in where they are, where they can go, and how to get back. Other aspects of information architecture benefit from purpose-built methods. Here's how to connect them.

For identifying Jobs, Tasks, and Activities (feeds Step 01): JTBD interview techniques (Moesta, Ulwick, Kalbach — see Appendix) give you the raw material that Step 01 structures. The quality of your Step 01 output depends directly on the quality of this upstream research.

For modeling entities and their relationships (runs alongside Steps 01–02): If your product manages persistent objects, run an Object-Oriented UX (OOUX) mapping in parallel. OOUX defines the entity model that Sightline's Entity-First anchor builds on.

For cataloging navigation system types (extends Step 05): Rosenfeld & Morville's IA taxonomy (global, local, contextual, supplemental) provides finer-grained navigation component classification after Step 05 gives you a paradigm composition.

For validating the result (extends Step 06): The audit tests structural intent. Pair it with usability testing — tree tests, first-click tests, task-based walkthroughs — to catch interaction-level problems the audit won't surface.

What Sightline Does Not Cover

A few things are explicitly out of scope. Sightline does not prescribe visual design, interaction patterns, or frontend implementation — those depend on your design system, platform constraints, and engineering stack. It does not address content strategy, search design, or accessibility requirements, each of which has its own established methodology. And it does not replace the user research that feeds it; if the upstream Jobs and Tasks are wrong, the navigation architecture will be wrong regardless of how rigorously you follow the steps.

The Companion Workbook

Each step in Sightline produces decisions and artifacts that feed the next step. The Sightline Workbook gives you a structured place to capture these as you go — your JTBD hierarchy, diagnostic scores, scope profile, role decisions, paradigm composition, and the resulting navigation outline. Open it alongside this document and fill in each section as the guide prompts you.

Step 01

Define

What are my users' Jobs to Be Done?

Users don't think in pages, menus, or modules. They think in outcomes they're trying to achieve. A navigation system should mirror that intent hierarchy — not your org chart, not your database schema, and not whatever your backend team decided to name the API endpoints.

Most users have 3–7 Jobs. If you're finding significantly more, you're probably classifying Tasks as Jobs. If you're finding fewer than 3, you may be grouping multiple Jobs under one umbrella. Your navigation system needs to accommodate all of them.

The Three Levels

Job
Changes: Rarely
The overarching outcome a user is trying to achieve. Emotionally resonant, context-independent, and product-independent. This is the why.
Changes when...
The user's role, goals, or life circumstances change. A promotion, a career shift, a new business model — these are the forces that redefine a Job.
Example
"Keep my team's financial health strong so we can grow sustainably" — true whether the user has your software, a spreadsheet, or a paper ledger.
Litmus Test
Ask: "Would this still be true if the product didn't exist?" Jobs are product-independent. If the statement only makes sense inside your product, it's probably a Task.
Task
Changes: Periodically
A discrete, goal-oriented unit of work that advances the Job. Has a clear start and end, requires deliberate effort, and can be planned or delegated.
Changes when...
Workflows, processes, or business needs evolve. New tools get adopted, teams restructure, compliance requirements shift.
Example
"Create and send monthly budget reports" might become "Set up automated reporting dashboards" — same Job, different Task.
Litmus Test
Ask: "Would someone put this on a to-do list?" Tasks are plannable. If you can imagine delegating it to a colleague with a brief explanation, it's a Task. If you'd have to point at the screen, it's an Activity.
Activity
Changes: Frequently
A concrete, atomic interaction within a Task. Happens in real-time, is tool-dependent, and represents the unit of UI interaction design.
Changes when...
The tool or interface is redesigned. A button becomes a drag target, a form becomes a wizard, a manual step gets automated.
Example
"Select date range, pull data, choose template, export PDF" — each would change if the reporting tool was redesigned.
Litmus Test 1
Ask: "Does this require a specific UI element to exist?" Activities are tool-dependent. If the action would change significantly with a redesigned interface, it's an Activity.
Litmus Test 2
Ask: "Would the user plan a session around doing this?" If they'd set aside time, it's a Task. The individual interactions within that session are Activities.
Key Insight

Jobs rarely change — they're tied to the user's role and goals. Tasks change periodically as workflows evolve. Activities change frequently, every time the interface is updated. Your navigation at each level should match this rate of change. If your primary navigation shifts every time you redesign a feature, you've mapped Activities at the Job level.

AI Connection

This hierarchy is also the semantic backbone that AI-assisted navigation needs to resolve user intent. When a user tells a copilot "show me the compliance report," the system needs to know that compliance reporting is a Task under a specific Job, and that the report itself is an Activity within that Task. A clean JTBD hierarchy makes both structural navigation and AI-assisted navigation work better — the two aren't competing investments.

A Note on Cross-Job Tasks

Some Tasks serve multiple Jobs. "Generate a financial report" might advance both "Keep stakeholders informed" and "Ensure regulatory compliance." Don't force these into a single Job — acknowledge them and move on. We'll address cross-Job Tasks in the Map step.

When Something Could Be a Job or a Task

The litmus tests above resolve most classifications, but borderline cases are common — especially for periodic, high-level activities like "Run quarterly compliance audit" or "Conduct monthly pipeline review." These pass the to-do list test (plannable, delegable) and feel outcome-oriented enough to be a Job. When you hit this ambiguity, apply these two tiebreakers in order.

Tiebreaker 1: Scope and Delegation
Can this be delegated as a whole to a different person or role, with the expectation that they'll figure out the sub-steps?
If yes — classify as a Job
It has enough scope to contain its own Tasks. Example: "Run quarterly compliance audit" — you'd assign this to a compliance lead and they'd determine which checks to run, which reports to pull, and what to flag.
If no — classify as a Task
It needs to be broken down before someone else could take it on. Example: "Scan license usage" — this is a specific action within a compliance audit or a reporting workflow. You'd give someone the instruction, not a mandate.
Tiebreaker 2: Independence from Trigger Context
Does this item mean the same thing regardless of what triggered it? Jobs are context-independent — "Ensure regulatory compliance" is the same Job whether triggered by an upcoming audit, a customer request, or a quarterly schedule. Tasks change meaning based on their parent Job — "Pull a usage report" means something different in a compliance context vs. a sales context.
If context-independent
Classify as a Job.
If meaning shifts with context
Classify as a Task, and note each Job it can belong to. These become cross-Job Tasks handled in Step 02's Job Gravity.
Exit Criterion

You're done with disambiguation when every item in your hierarchy has exactly one classification (Job, Task, or Activity) that you can defend with a specific tiebreaker. If you've spent more than 15 minutes on a single item, it's almost certainly a Task that feels important enough to be a Job — classify it as a Task under the Job it most frequently serves, and move on. Its importance will be captured by frequency annotation below, not by its hierarchical level.

Annotating Frequency and Criticality

After defining your Job → Task → Activity hierarchy, annotate each Task with two dimensions. This data feeds directly into Step 02 (as a diagnostic tiebreaker), Step 05 (as a paradigm constraint), and Step 06 (as audit input for Frequency Overrides Hierarchy).

DimensionValuesHow to determine
FrequencyDaily · Weekly · Monthly · QuarterlyHow often does a typical user perform this Task? Use session logs if available. If not, ask: "If we removed this Task from the product for a week, how many users would notice and when?"
CriticalityBlocking · Important · SupportingBlocking = failure to complete stops other work or causes business harm. Important = needed for the Job to succeed, but delay is tolerable. Supporting = useful but not required for Job completion.
Example annotations

Job: Advance sprint deliverables

Task: "Prepare daily standup notes" — Frequency: Daily, Criticality: Supporting (keeps team aligned, but no work is blocked if missed)

Task: "Review and approve pull requests" — Frequency: Daily, Criticality: Blocking (unreviewed PRs stall merges)

Task: "Run retrospective" — Frequency: Quarterly (per sprint cycle), Criticality: Supporting

Defining for Multiple User Types

Most products serve more than one persona. A project management tool serves Project Managers, Developers, and Designers — each with overlapping but distinct Job maps. Don't try to define all personas simultaneously. Instead:

Define one persona at a time. Give each persona its own complete Job → Task → Activity hierarchy. This keeps each map clean and prevents premature averaging.

Then overlay the maps. Once you have 2–3 individual maps, lay them side by side. Look for: shared Jobs, shared Tasks under different Jobs, and persona-exclusive Jobs. Mark these overlaps and divergences — they feed the role-based decisions in Step 04.

Step 02

Map

How does intent translate to navigation tiers?

You've defined your users' Jobs, Tasks, and Activities. Now you need to map them to navigation tiers. But first, answer a foundational question: what organizes your user's mental model?

The answer determines how your JTBD hierarchy translates to navigation structure. Get this wrong and your navigation will fight the user's thinking — even if every individual component is well-designed.

Choosing Your Navigation Anchor

Users tend to organize their work around one of four dominant mental models. Most products are hybrids. Typically one model dominates — identify it and let it drive your primary navigation. When no clear dominant emerges, the hybrid resolver below will help you structure the split.

Process-first
Jobs = Primary nav
The user thinks in distinct phases or workspaces, each with its own tools and mindset. They say: "I'm in research mode" or "I'm doing my weekly review."
Diagnostic
Do users describe their work as distinct modes or phases? Does each phase involve different tools and a different mindset?
Examples
TurboTax — linear flow through phases. Shopify Admin — Products, Orders, Marketing as distinct workspaces. HubSpot — Marketing, Sales, Service as separate hubs.
MAPPING JOB / Phase ——→ Primary Navigation Always visible "Research" / "Delivery" Top bar, sidebar root | v TASK ——→ Secondary Navigation Within phase context "Run customer interviews" Tabs, sub-sections | v ACTIVITY ——→ In-Page Navigation Contextual / transient "Schedule interview call" Buttons, forms, inline actions
Entity-first
Entities = Primary nav
The user tracks specific things — features, accounts, projects, cases — that persist over time. They say: "How's the Johnson account doing?" The entity is the anchor; Jobs become lenses on that entity.
Diagnostic
Do users refer to their work by name? Do they track entities through multiple Jobs over time? Is their primary question "what's the status of X?"
Examples
Salesforce — Accounts, Contacts, Opportunities as primary objects. Jira — Issues move through workflows. GitHub — Repositories with Code, Issues, PRs as tabs.
MAPPING ENTITY ——→ Primary Navigation Always visible "Feature X" / "Acme Acct" Entity list, entity detail | v JOB / Phase ——→ Secondary Navigation Tabs within entity "Discovery" / "Delivery" Phase tabs, concern tabs | v TASK → ACTIVITY ——→ In-Page Navigation Contextual / transient "Write requirements" Forms, editors, inline actions
Queue/Inbox-first
Incoming stream = Primary nav
The user's work arrives as a stream of items that need processing. The user doesn't go find work; work comes to them. Their first question is "what should I work on next?"
Diagnostic
Does work arrive continuously rather than being initiated by the user? Is the primary interaction processing items from a list? Are items transient (resolved and archived) rather than persistent?
Examples
Zendesk — ticket queues filtered by status and priority. Gmail — inbox with labels and smart categories. Slack — message streams by channel. PagerDuty — incident queue.
MAPPING QUEUE VIEWS ——→ Primary Navigation Always visible "My Queue" / "Unassigned" Filtered lists, inbox views | v ITEM DETAIL ——→ Secondary Navigation Tabs within item "Conversation" / "History" Concern tabs, detail panels | v ACTIVITY ——→ In-Page Navigation Contextual / transient "Reply" / "Escalate" Buttons, quick actions, shortcuts
Activity/Mode-first
Dominant activity = Separate mode
The user's core work requires deep, immersive focus. The product has two worlds: an immersive creation mode and a management/browse mode, connected by an explicit mode-switch.
Diagnostic
Is there one dominant activity that requires full-screen focus? Does that activity account for the majority of usage time?
Examples
Figma — File browser ↔ Canvas. VS Code — Editor dominates. Substack/Ghost — Full-screen editor for writing, dashboard for everything else.
MAPPING MANAGEMENT MODE ——→ Standard Navigation Top bar, sidebar "Browse projects, check Jobs and entities organized metrics, manage settings" using process or entity model ↑↓ explicit mode-switch IMMERSIVE MODE ——→ Minimal / Hidden Navigation Reduced or none "Write the article, Full-screen focus, contextual design the interface" toolbars only, nav recedes
Reactive vs. Proactive Work

Queue/Inbox-first is the model for reactive workflows — the system surfaces what needs attention. The other three assume proactive workflows — the user initiates navigation toward their goals. Reactive navigation needs to bring work to the user; proactive navigation needs to let the user find work. Many products blend both.

Most products are hybrids

Pure instances of any single model are rare. Salesforce is entity-dominant with process elements. A support tool might be queue-first for agents but entity-first for managers. The goal isn't to pick one model exclusively — it's to identify which model dominates and let it drive your primary navigation, then layer secondary models into lower tiers.

Identifying Your Dominant Model

Run these four diagnostic checks at the session level — a session being one continuous period of product use, from the moment a user opens or switches to your product to the moment they leave. Scope each diagnostic to how users begin, move through, and end that session. If three or more point to the same model, you have a clear dominant anchor.

1. Listen to the Language
Scan support tickets, user interviews, or session recordings for how users describe their sessions and goals — not their moment-to-moment interactions. The nouns and verbs people reach for when explaining what they came to do and why reveal the anchor.
Process-first
"I'm in my weekly review" / "I need to do my month-end close"
Entity-first
"I need to check on the Anderson account" / "What's happening with Project Alpha?"
Queue-first
"Let me see what came in overnight" / "What's next in the queue?"
Activity-first
"I need to finish this design" / "Let me get back to writing"
2. Map What Triggers a Session
Categorize why users open the product at the session level — what prompts them to sit down and start. The trigger tells you what the user is optimizing for when they first arrive, which is exactly what primary navigation needs to serve.
Process-first
A recurring ritual — "every Monday I do X"
Entity-first
A status check — "I need to see where this thing stands"
Queue-first
A notification or alert — something happened and needs a response
Activity-first
A block of time — "I have two hours to create"
3. Look at Session Shape
Observe the overall arc of a typical session, not individual clicks. If you have analytics, session duration and page-transition patterns reveal this empirically. If not, five minutes of observation will usually do it. You're looking for the shape of the session — linear, deep-dive, burst-processing, or immersive — not the sequence of individual actions.
Process-first
Linear or semi-linear — enter a phase, do the work, move to the next phase
Entity-first
Deep on one object — find the thing, examine it from multiple angles, act on it
Queue-first
Short bursts across many items — scan, pick, process, next, next, next
Activity-first
Long immersive stretches — enter focused mode, stay there, minimal navigation
4. The Blank Screen Test
Forget the current UI entirely. If the user opened a blank screen and could type or say what they want, what would they ask for first? You're looking for their session-level goal, not a specific action. This strips away the bias of how the product is currently built and gets to underlying intent.
Process-first
"Start my month-end close"
Entity-first
"Take me to Project Alpha"
Queue-first
"Show me my tickets"
Activity-first
"Open the editor"

When Diagnostics Split: Resolving Ties and Hybrids

A clean 3-of-4 or 4-of-4 result is ideal, but many products — especially enterprise tools, agile workflows, and multi-role platforms — will produce a 2/2 split or a 3/1 with a strong dissenting signal. The product genuinely serves multiple mental models, and the navigation needs to account for that explicitly rather than forcing a single anchor.

Score the diagnostics. Assign each of the four diagnostics (Language, Session Trigger, Session Shape, Blank Screen) to the model it most clearly supports. If a diagnostic genuinely could go either way, mark it as split rather than forcing it into one column. Count the results:

ResultInterpretationAction
4–0 or 3–0 (with 1 split)Clear dominant modelProceed with that model as your primary anchor. No hybrid needed.
3–1Dominant model with a secondary signalPrimary anchor is the 3-count model. Check whether the dissenting diagnostic points to a high-frequency Task (use frequency data from Step 01). If so, that Task earns primary-level placement regardless of model — apply Frequency Overrides Hierarchy preemptively.
2–2Genuine hybridExpand the section below.
2–1–1 or flatterNo clear modelInsufficient signal. Gather more user data before proceeding — five contextual interviews focused on "walk me through your last session" will typically resolve the ambiguity.
Resolving a 2/2 Split

When two models tie, the split almost always falls into one of three patterns. Identify which one matches your product and apply the corresponding resolution.

Pattern 1: Entity + Process Split
Users track persistent objects and work in distinct phases. Common in: project management, CRM, product development tools.
Diagnostic signature
Language and Blank Screen point to entities ("Show me Project Alpha"). Session Trigger and Session Shape point to process ("I'm doing sprint planning").
Resolution
Entity-primary with process as secondary tier. Entities persist in the primary rail; process phases appear as tabs or views within each entity. The exception: if session-initiating process Tasks (daily standup, weekly review) account for >30% of sessions, elevate those Tasks to the primary rail as pinned shortcuts alongside entity items.
Worked example

An agile PM tool where diagnostics split: users reference "tickets" and "stories" (entity) but sessions follow sprint phases (process). Resolution: primary rail lists entities (Backlog, Active Sprint, Issues) but pins the daily-frequency Task "Standup Prep" as a top-level shortcut, preventing it from being buried two clicks deep inside Active Sprint.

Pattern 2: Entity + Queue Split
Users manage persistent objects and process incoming streams. Common in: IT service management, customer support platforms, asset management.
Diagnostic signature
Language and Blank Screen reference named entities ("Check asset SN-4421"). Session Trigger and Session Shape show queue behavior (notifications arrive, user processes items sequentially).
Resolution
Determine which model drives the majority of session time (not session count). If users spend most of their time processing queued items with brief entity lookups, queue-primary with entity as secondary. If users spend most time deep in entity detail with periodic queue checks, entity-primary with a persistent queue widget or pinned queue view in the primary rail.
Worked example

An ITAM tool where assets are the core entity but maintenance requests arrive as a queue. Maintenance technicians spend 60% of session time processing the request queue. Resolution: primary rail includes both entity navigation (Assets, Contracts, Vendors) and a co-equal "Maintenance Queue" filtered-list view. The queue is not subordinated under an entity — it earns its own primary slot because it drives the majority of active work time.

Pattern 3: Process + Queue Split
Users follow structured workflows and respond to incoming items. Common in: healthcare workflows, editorial/publishing, incident response.
Diagnostic signature
Session Shape shows linear phases. Session Trigger shows reactive (notification-driven) starts.
Resolution
Process-primary with queue as an entry point. The queue acts as the session-start surface (what needs attention now?), and selecting an item routes into the appropriate process phase. The primary rail organizes by process; the landing page or home view shows the queue.
Worked example

An editorial platform where articles arrive as submissions (queue) but follow a review pipeline (process: Triage → Edit → Fact-check → Publish). Resolution: primary rail shows process phases. Dashboard/home shows the incoming queue. Selecting a queued item opens it in the appropriate phase context.

The 30% Rule for Hybrid Elevation

When resolving a hybrid, use this threshold: if the secondary model drives ≥30% of sessions or ≥30% of active session time, it earns co-equal primary placement rather than being subordinated to the secondary tier. Below 30%, the secondary model can be served through pinned shortcuts, filtered views, or command palette without needing its own primary-level anchor.

For a quantitative scoring approach to the diagnostics, see the Diagnostic Scoring Matrix in Common Questions.

AI Connection

AI-assisted navigation doesn't eliminate the need for a dominant mental model — it depends on one. When a copilot resolves "show me the dashboard," it needs to know whether "dashboard" means a process-phase summary, an entity overview, a queue triage view, or a mode within a creation tool. Your anchor tells the AI how to interpret ambiguous intent. Without it, the AI guesses — and guesses differently each time.

Mapping Principles (Apply to All Models)

Persistence mirrors stability. Whatever occupies your primary navigation should be the most stable element of the experience. It should rarely change between releases.

Screen real estate mirrors scope. The primary anchor earns the most persistent screen space. Secondary concerns live inside that anchor's territory. In-page interactions live inside the page.

Interaction cost mirrors commitment. Switching between primary-level items is a significant context shift. Switching between secondary items should be lightweight. In-page activities should require near-zero navigation.

The Persistence Test

If a navigation element disappears when it shouldn't, or persists when it's irrelevant, you've got a level mismatch. Legitimate exceptions: immersive modes hide primary nav deliberately; queue-first detail views may collapse the queue to a panel.

Step 03

Scope

What are my product's navigation constraints?

Before assessing behavioral complexity or selecting paradigms, profile your product across six dimensions. Your answers directly constrain which patterns will work and which will break.

1. How many top-level Jobs does your product support?
≤5 Jobs: Expose all in a single layer — top bar or bottom tabs.
5–15 Jobs: Side navigation with groupings, or a hub with categorized entry points. Consider command palette as an accelerator.
15+ Jobs: Compound approach — hub-and-spoke plus search, or role-based filtering.
2. How deep is your Task tree?
1 level: Tabs or segmented controls within each Job. No breadcrumbs needed.
2 levels: Collapsible sidebar sections. Consider breadcrumbs as a supplement.
3+ levels: Sidebar for top two levels; breadcrumbs for the rest. Without breadcrumbs, users will get lost.
3. How varied is your user expertise?
Uniform: One navigation system. Optimize for the dominant expertise level.
Moderate spread: Structural nav for everyone, command palette for power users. Role-aware defaults help.
Extreme spread: Layered navigation — visible structural nav for discovery, keyboard shortcuts for speed, guided flows for onboarding.
4. How concentrated is usage?
Highly concentrated (~80% of sessions involve 2–3 Tasks): Elevate those Tasks to Job-level prominence.
Evenly distributed: Navigation needs equal weight across many options — side nav or hub patterns.
Mixed (daily + quarterly Tasks): Quarterly Tasks can't be buried. Search and command palette become safety nets.
5. How much navigation learning will users tolerate?
Near-zero: Must be immediately obvious. Familiar patterns only — top tabs, bottom bars, visible labels.
Moderate: Users will invest time if the product is central to their work. Richer patterns with good onboarding.
High: Mission-critical, daily use. Training budgets exist. Optimize for power-user efficiency.
6. What platforms does your product serve?
Desktop-only: Full access to side nav, hover states, keyboard shortcuts, multi-panel layouts.
Responsive web: Your composition formula needs a responsive strategy. Side nav collapses; hover interactions need touch alternatives.
Native mobile: Platform conventions apply. iOS favors bottom tabs; Android favors top tabs and drawers. Command palette becomes pull-down search.
Multi-platform: Two composition formulas sharing a JTBD hierarchy but using different paradigms per platform.
What to do with your answers

Your profile across these six dimensions acts as a filter for Step 05. You'll use it there to narrow which paradigms are viable and resolve conflicts between competing dimensions.

Step 04

Assess

How do roles and context shape the navigation?

Three behavioral dimensions constrain your paradigm options: role-based flexing at the Job level, adaptive flexing at the Task and Activity levels, and user context that shapes how every tier should feel.

Role-Based Navigation

In Step 01, you defined Job maps for each persona and noted overlaps and divergences. Now you'll use that overlap data to choose a role-based navigation approach.

ApproachHow it worksWhen to useOverlapRisk
Separate appsEach role gets its own application.Roles share <20% of Jobs.<30% shared JobsMaintenance doubles.
Role-filteredOne nav, items shown/hidden by role.Roles share core Jobs but differ in access.30–70% shared JobsGhost nav — users sense hidden items.
Role-aware defaultsSame nav, different starting points by role.Same Jobs, different frequency patterns.>70% shared JobsLowest risk.

Start with role-aware defaults and only escalate when the data demands it. Consider separate apps when Job maps overlap less than roughly 20% — below that point, a shared navigation structure creates more confusion than it solves. To determine your overlap ratio, lay your persona Job maps side by side and count shared vs. exclusive Jobs — the percentage of shared Jobs tells you which row of the table above applies.

The Cardinal Rule

Never remove navigation options silently. If something will disappear, move, or change, the user needs to understand why. Adaptive navigation must be transparently adaptive.

Composing Your Navigation System

Individual paradigms are building blocks. A well-composed system combines them across four slots. Tension arises when two paradigms compete for the same slot.

The Composition Formula

[Primary] + [Secondary] + [Depth] + [Accelerators]

One paradigm per structural slot. Two in the same slot creates tension that needs explicit resolution (see hybrid patterns in Step 02 and the 30% Rule). Empty primary or secondary = gap. Depth and accelerators are optional.

Composition Examples

Salesforce (entity-first): Side nav + Tabs + Breadcrumbs + command palette

Instagram (process-first): Bottom tab bar + Full-screen pages + None + Contextual actions

Figma (activity-first): Mode-switch + Side panel + None + command palette + Contextual toolbar

Zendesk (queue-first): Work queue + Tabs + None + Macros + Keyboard shortcuts

Worked Compositions: Enterprise B2B

Consumer and light-SaaS examples illustrate paradigms, but enterprise B2B tools introduce complications the simpler examples don't surface: hybrid anchors, deep hierarchies, multi-role navigation, and cross-Job Task gravity. Here are two fully worked compositions.

IT Asset Management (Entity + Queue Hybrid)
Anchor
Entity-primary / Queue co-primary (30% rule: maintenance queue drives >30% of session time)
Scope
6–8 Jobs, 2–3 level depth, moderate expertise, mixed usage, desktop + responsive
Composition
Primary: Side navigation with two sections — entity items (Assets, Contracts, Vendors, Licenses) and a co-equal "Maintenance Queue" filtered-list view. Secondary: Tab bar within entity detail (Overview, History, Related Items, Compliance). Depth: Breadcrumbs (Assets → Asset SN-4421 → Maintenance History). Accelerators: command palette for cross-entity search, pinned shortcut for "New Request" (Daily + Blocking).
Why this works
The queue earns primary placement because maintenance technicians spend the majority of session time there. Entity nav persists alongside it rather than containing it. Cross-Job Tasks ("Run compliance audit") get a pinned shortcut in the primary rail rather than being buried under an entity. The secondary tab bar within entities mirrors the process phases (lifecycle stages) without forcing a process-primary structure.
Agile Project Management (Entity + Process Hybrid)
Anchor
Process-primary with entity as secondary (scoring: Process 8.5, Entity 5.5). Daily standup prep elevated to primary via Frequency Overrides Hierarchy.
Scope
4 Jobs, 2-level depth, moderate expertise, concentrated usage (standup + board dominate), responsive web
Composition
Primary: Side navigation organized by process phase — "Standup" (pinned, Daily + Blocking), Board, Backlog, Reports, Retrospectives. Secondary: Tab bar within each phase (Board view: Board | List | Timeline). Depth: Breadcrumbs (Active Sprint → Story-123 → Subtask). Accelerators: command palette for ticket lookup by ID, contextual actions on cards.
Why this works
Process-primary because session shape follows sprint phases. Standup earns a dedicated primary slot despite being a Task, not a Job — its Daily + Blocking frequency annotation overrides hierarchy. Retrospectives stays in the primary rail despite 10x lower frequency because it maps to a distinct Job (process improvement), not because of frequency. The entity layer (individual tickets) lives in secondary tabs and depth navigation, which matches how users interact with entities: within the context of whatever sprint phase they're in.

Primary Structure

Process-first: ≤5 Jobs → top nav. 5–15 → side nav. 15+ → hub & spoke.
Entity-first: Side nav or list view.
Queue-first: Work queue with thin sidebar for view switching.
Activity-first: The mode-switch mechanism itself.

Secondary Structure

Tab bar: 3–5 parallel options within the primary anchor.
Collapsible sub-sections: 5+ items.
Mutually exclusive at the same level.

Depth Handling (conditional)

Only needed beyond 2 levels. Breadcrumb + drill-down is the standard depth handler. Skip this for shallow products.

Accelerators (additive)

Command palette for users who know what they want. Contextual actions for Activity-level interactions. Both can coexist. Neither counts toward structural complexity.

AI Connection

AI-assisted navigation — copilots, conversational agents, intent-driven routing — fits in the accelerator slot. It extends the command palette pattern with natural language: instead of typing a known command, the user describes what they need. Like all accelerators, it layers on top of structural navigation rather than replacing it. The critical design constraint: if the AI layer is unavailable, slow, or returns a wrong result, the user must still be able to reach any destination through the primary and secondary structure. AI is a fast path, not the only path.

Scope Conflicts: When Dimensions Collide

Your Step 03 scope profile acts as a filter here. A single dimension rarely rules out a paradigm on its own — it's specific combinations that force decisions. Below are the most common dimension conflicts and how to resolve them.

Many Jobs + Responsive Web
5+ top-level Jobs on responsive web.
Conflict
Side navigation is the natural fit for 5+ Jobs, but a persistent sidebar consumes too much horizontal space on tablet and mobile viewports.
Rule
Side nav is viable only if you define an explicit collapse strategy before finalizing your composition. Specify: (a) the breakpoint at which the sidebar collapses, (b) the collapsed state (hamburger drawer, bottom tab subset, or off-canvas), and (c) which Jobs remain visible in the collapsed state (use frequency annotations from Step 01 — Daily/Blocking Tasks stay visible). If you can't define the collapsed state, Hub & Spoke is the safer choice.
Deep Hierarchy + Low Learning Tolerance
3+ Task tree levels with near-zero navigation learning tolerance.
Conflict
Deep hierarchies require breadcrumbs and drill-down, but users with low tolerance won't invest in learning a multi-level structure. Nested accordions are especially hostile in this profile.
Rule
Flatten the visible hierarchy to 2 levels maximum. Use breadcrumbs for depth beyond level 2, but ensure the primary and secondary tiers are visible and labeled at all times. Add command palette or search as a mandatory escape hatch — users who can't find something structurally must have a text-based alternative.
Concentrated Usage + Evenly Distributed Jobs
80% of sessions touch 2–3 Tasks, but the product supports 6+ equally important Jobs.
Conflict
The high-frequency Tasks want top-level placement, but the Job structure demands equal weight across many items. Pinning 3 Tasks to the top of a 10-item side nav makes the other 7 feel secondary even when they're architecturally equal.
Rule
Separate the shortcut layer from the structural layer. Use a "Quick Actions" or "Pinned" section at the top of the primary nav (or as a persistent dashboard widget) for the high-frequency Tasks, then show the full Job structure below or alongside. This preserves architectural equality while giving daily workflows a fast path.
Extreme Expertise Spread + Native Mobile
Users range from first-day novices to decade-long experts, on mobile.
Conflict
Mobile has limited screen real estate for layered navigation strategies. You can't fit visible structural nav, shortcuts, and guided onboarding on a 375px-wide screen simultaneously.
Rule
Default to the novice experience (visible labels, standard tab bar, no hidden gestures). Serve experts through progressive shortcuts: long-press for quick actions, pull-down search, and customizable tab bar items. Avoid hamburger menus as primary nav for novice-heavy audiences — bottom tabs with 4–5 items plus a "More" overflow is the ceiling.
Multi-Platform + Deep Hierarchy
Desktop and mobile apps with 3+ levels of Task depth.
Conflict
The desktop composition formula (side nav + breadcrumbs + drill-down) doesn't translate to mobile. Breadcrumbs are effectively unusable on mobile viewports.
Rule
Write two composition formulas that share the same JTBD hierarchy and navigation anchor but use different paradigms. Desktop: side nav + breadcrumbs. Mobile: bottom tabs (top 2 levels) + stacked drill-down (depth). The shared anchor ensures conceptual consistency; the different paradigms respect platform constraints.

Audit

How do I know my navigation works?

Seven principles for auditing your navigation system. Each includes conceptual framing, a decision question, an implementation example, and a testable pass/fail criterion — the specific measurement that determines whether your navigation outline satisfies the principle.

Conceptual (Designers, PMs)
Decision (Practitioners)
Implementation (Developers)
Test (Pass/Fail Criterion)

Principle 1: Frequency Overrides Hierarchy

Conceptual

If 80% of sessions involve the same Task, that Task earns Job-level prominence — regardless of logical position.

Decision

Question: Are your 3 highest-frequency Tasks reachable in ≤1 click?

Heuristic: >40% of sessions = persistent shortcut.

Implementation

"Create Invoice" lives under Finance → Billing → Invoices, but it's the most-used Task. Give it a "+" button in the header, a command palette shortcut, and a pinned sidebar entry.

Test

Data needed: Step 01 frequency annotations.

Method: List every Task annotated as Daily frequency. For each, count clicks from the product's landing state to that Task.

Pass: Every Daily + Blocking Task is reachable in ≤1 click. Every Daily + Important Task is reachable in ≤2 clicks.

Fail: Any Daily + Blocking Task requires ≥2 clicks.

Principle 2: Stability Matches Persistence

Conceptual

Top nav should change as rarely as your Jobs — almost never. In-page controls can change every release.

Decision

Question: Has top-level nav changed in 6 months?

Heuristic: Primary nav ≤1x/year. Secondary quarterly. In-page every sprint.

Implementation

GitHub's top nav (Code, Issues, PRs, Actions, Projects) has been stable for years. New features add within sections, not alongside them.

Test

Data needed: Git history or release notes for the past 12 months (existing product) or the navigation outline's tier assignments (new product).

Method: For existing products, count how many times primary nav items were added, removed, or renamed. For new products, check whether any item in the primary tier maps to something that changes with feature releases (Activities, specific Task workflows) rather than stable Jobs.

Pass: Primary nav changed ≤1x in 12 months; all primary items map to Jobs or to Tasks that earned primary placement through Principle 1 (frequency override).

Fail: Primary nav changed ≥2x, or contains Task/Activity-level items that lack a frequency-based justification for their placement.

Principle 3: Context Must Be Preserved

Conceptual

Switching Tasks within a Job should preserve state. It should feel like switching tabs, not closing one app and opening another.

Decision

Question: Task A → Task B → Task A — is state preserved?

Heuristic: If switching causes data loss or scroll reset, it's broken.

Implementation

Notion: switching database views preserves filters, sort order, and scroll position. The lens changes; the context persists.

Test

Data needed: Navigation outline from Step 05.

Method: For every pair of sibling Tasks within a Job, trace the transition: Task A → Task B → Task A. Identify what state is at risk (form inputs, scroll position, filter selections, unsaved work).

Pass: All sibling-Task transitions preserve user-created state or prompt before discarding it.

Fail: Any sibling transition silently resets state (scroll position, filters, form data).

Principle 4: Progressive Disclosure, Not Confusion

Conceptual

Don't expose Activity-level nav until the user commits to a Task. Each tier reveals the next. But the user must always see which Job and Task they're inside.

Decision

Question: At the deepest point, can the user tell which Job and Task they're inside?

Heuristic: If only a page title is visible — add breadcrumbs.

Implementation

Stripe: sidebar shows Job section, header shows Task entity, breadcrumbs show depth. All three tiers visible simultaneously.

Test

Data needed: Navigation outline.

Method: Navigate to the deepest point in the hierarchy. Check: (1) Can you identify the current Job without scrolling? (2) Can you identify the current Task? (3) Are the adjacent options (sibling Tasks, parent Job) visible or one click away?

Pass: All three tiers are identifiable from the deepest point.

Fail: Only page title is visible with no breadcrumb, sidebar highlight, or other tier indicator.

Principle 5: Escape Velocity

Conceptual

From any point, a user should reach any higher level in ≤2 interactions.

Decision

Question: How many clicks from deepest point to Job level?

Heuristic: ≤2 = good. 3 = okay with breadcrumbs. 4+ = broken.

Implementation

Three escape mechanisms: persistent sidebar, un-truncated breadcrumbs, command palette. Most complex products need at least two.

Test

Data needed: Navigation outline with depth measurements.

Method: From the deepest point in each Job's hierarchy, count interactions required to reach the Job level (primary nav). An interaction = one click, one keyboard shortcut, or one gesture.

Pass: ≤2 interactions for every path.

Fail: Any path requires ≥4 interactions. Borderline (3 interactions) passes only if breadcrumbs are present.

Principle 6: Navigation as Wayfinding

Conceptual

At every moment, navigation must answer three questions. If any is unanswerable, the system is broken.

Decision

Three questions your navigation must answer at every moment:

1. Where am I? → Active states, breadcrumbs, page titles.

2. Where can I go? → Visible nav options, available actions.

3. How do I get back? → Back buttons, breadcrumbs, persistent nav.

If any answer is unclear at any point, the navigation system is broken.

Implementation

"Where am I?" — Active sidebar highlighted. "Where can I go?" — Sibling Tasks as tabs. "How do I get back?" — Clickable breadcrumb, persistent sidebar, working browser back.

Test

Data needed: Navigation outline or prototype.

Method: Pick 5 random screens. For each, answer the three wayfinding questions without interacting with the page (visual inspection only).

Pass: All three questions answerable on all 5 screens.

Fail: Any question unanswerable on any screen. Common failure: "How do I get back?" unanswerable when breadcrumbs are absent and sidebar doesn't show active state.

Principle 7: Navigation Must Feel Instant

Conceptual

Structural correctness means nothing if navigation feels slow. Every transition should feel like it happens in response to the user's action, not after it.

Decision

Question: Do your three highest-frequency transitions complete in under 200ms perceived time?

Heuristic: Primary switches <100ms. Secondary <300ms. Depth transitions <500ms with a loading indicator within 100ms.

Implementation

Optimistic UI for tab switches. Preload adjacent views on hover. Skeleton screens for deeper transitions. Linear and Notion pre-cache sidebar items so page switches feel instant.

Test

Data needed: Prototype or production build with browser dev tools.

Method: Measure perceived transition time for the 3 highest-frequency navigation actions (identified by Step 01 frequency annotations). Use Performance tab or stopwatch timing.

Pass: Primary nav switches <100ms. Secondary switches <300ms. Depth transitions show a loading indicator within 100ms and complete within 500ms.

Fail: Any of the 3 highest-frequency transitions exceeds its tier's threshold.

AI Resilience Check

If your product includes AI-assisted navigation (copilot, conversational agent, intent routing), apply one additional audit: remove the AI layer entirely and attempt to complete your three highest-frequency Tasks using only structural navigation. If any Task becomes unreachable or requires more than two extra clicks, your structural navigation has a gap that the AI was masking. The AI layer should make navigation faster, not make it possible.

Common Questions

Edge cases and ambiguities that come up when applying Sightline to real products.

A Task looks the same in two different Jobs — do I need Job Gravity?

It depends on whether the user is asking the same question or a different question.

Same Task, multiple Jobs: "Generate a financial report" is the same work from either Stakeholder Reporting or Compliance. Job Gravity applies: pick a canonical home, add pointers.

Same Activity, different Task context: "Review analytics" during Discovery and during Measurement involve the same screen but different questions. These are different Tasks — design each view for its context. No Job Gravity needed.

How do I handle navigation for AI-assisted features?

AI fits in the accelerator slot of your composition formula. Apply the AI Resilience Check in your audit. For the full argument on why structural navigation remains essential alongside AI, see What About AI?

How does the Diagnostic Scoring Matrix work?

This is an optional quantitative supplement to the decision tree in Step 02. Use it when the qualitative patterns don't clearly match your situation, or when you need a defensible numerical rationale for stakeholders.

How to score: For each diagnostic, assign a confidence score from 0 to 3 for each of the four models. 0 = no signal, 1 = weak signal, 2 = moderate signal, 3 = strong signal. A single diagnostic can give partial scores to multiple models (e.g., Language might score Entity 2, Process 1 if users reference both entities and phases but lean toward entities).

DiagnosticWeightProcessEntityQueueActivity
Language×1.00–30–30–30–3
Session Trigger×1.50–30–30–30–3
Session Shape×1.50–30–30–30–3
Blank Screen×1.00–30–30–30–3

Why Session Trigger and Session Shape are weighted higher: They reflect observable behavior rather than self-reported framing. Language and Blank Screen capture how users talk about their work; Trigger and Shape capture how they actually behave. When the two diverge, behavior is the more reliable navigation signal.

Reading the results:

Clear dominant (highest score ≥1.5× the runner-up): Use that model as your sole primary anchor.

Strong lean (highest score is 1.2–1.5× the runner-up): Primary anchor is the leader. Check whether the runner-up model drives any Task with daily-or-higher frequency. If so, apply Frequency Overrides Hierarchy: that Task earns primary-level placement even though its model isn't dominant.

Near-tie (highest score is <1.2× the runner-up): Genuine hybrid. Identify which of the three hybrid patterns in Step 02 matches, and apply the corresponding resolution. If none match, use the 30% Rule: whichever secondary model drives ≥30% of sessions or session time earns co-equal primary placement.

Worked example: Agile PM tool

Language: Entity 2, Process 1 (users say "ticket" more than "sprint planning," but both appear). Session Trigger: Process 3 (sessions start with "time for standup" or "sprint review day"). Session Shape: Process 2, Entity 1 (sessions follow sprint-phase patterns but include deep entity dives). Blank Screen: Entity 2 (users would say "show me PROJ-142").

Weighted scores — Process: (1×1) + (3×1.5) + (2×1.5) + (0×1) = 8.5. Entity: (2×1) + (0×1.5) + (1×1.5) + (2×1) = 5.5. Ratio: 8.5/5.5 = 1.55 — clear dominant. Process-primary, with entity detail as secondary tier. High-frequency entity Tasks (daily standup prep around specific tickets) earn primary shortcuts via Frequency Overrides Hierarchy.

Origins & Further Reading

Sightline draws from two intellectual traditions.

Jobs to Be Done (The Job Level)

JTBD theory holds that users "hire" products to make progress. The Job is the fundamental unit — stable, emotionally resonant, product-independent.

Clayton Christensen popularized JTBD for innovation strategy. Tony Ulwick (Outcome-Driven Innovation) systematized it into a quantitative methodology. Bob Moesta (The Switch Framework) focuses on emotional dimensions — his work directly informs the user context section in Step 04.

Hierarchical Task Analysis (The Task & Activity Levels)

HTA (Annett & Duncan, 1967) decomposes work into goals, sub-goals, operations, and plans. Activity Theory (Kaptelinin & Nardi) extends this with attention to tools and social dynamics.

Moesta — demandside.com · Demand-Side Sales 101

Getting Buy-In for a Navigation Sprint

Navigation breaks silently. Users can't find features, onboarding drags, and support tickets pile up around "how do I..." questions — but the root cause is rarely obvious because no one made a wrong decision. The decisions just weren't connected to each other.

Sightline is a structured method that connects what users are trying to accomplish to how they navigate your product. It produces a navigation architecture — documented, auditable, and anchored to user intent rather than the feature roadmap. The architecture survives feature launches, new user roles, and platform additions because it's grounded in what changes slowly (user goals) rather than what changes constantly (the interface).

The process adds roughly one design sprint to the project timeline. The return is a navigation structure that doesn't need to be redesigned every quarter.

What About AI?

A reasonable question before investing in navigation architecture: does structural navigation still matter when AI can take users directly where they need to go?

The argument is real, not hypothetical. Intent-driven interfaces are shipping now. A user says "show me last quarter's compliance report" and the system resolves that to the right screen, the right filters, the right data — no sidebar, no breadcrumbs, no hierarchy to learn. Command palettes, conversational copilots, and contextual AI agents are all moving in this direction. The pattern is accelerating across enterprise and consumer products alike.

If you're evaluating whether to read further, here's the short version: AI changes which navigation decisions matter, not whether navigation decisions matter. Structural navigation breaks down in four specific ways that AI does not currently solve — and in most cases, cannot solve by design.

Where Structural Navigation Remains Essential

Discoverability and serendipity. Conversational interfaces are request-response — they answer what you ask. Structural navigation supports browsing, which is how users discover capabilities they didn't know existed. A user scanning a sidebar might notice "Audit Log" and realize "I didn't know we had that." No one types "show me features I don't know about" into a chat. The gap between requesting and exploring is a real cost when it disappears.

Failure recovery. When AI routes a user to the wrong place — and the error rate doesn't need to be high for this to matter — the user needs a structural fallback. Without one, a bad AI response leaves them in a dead end with no option except rephrasing the query. Structural navigation is deterministic: same item, same destination, every time. That predictability is what makes recovery possible. The more critical the workflow, the more this matters.

Shared reference. "Go to Settings, then Integrations, then Webhooks" is a stable address that works in a support ticket, a training video, a Slack message, or an onboarding doc. "Ask the AI to take you to the webhook configuration" produces variable results depending on context, permissions, and model behavior. Navigation creates a shared vocabulary between users, support teams, and documentation. Remove the structure and you lose the ability to give directions.

Mental model formation. Navigation doesn't just take users somewhere — it teaches them what the product is. A sidebar with six items communicates "this system does six things, and they're roughly equal in importance." Remove that structure, and users can request what they already know to ask for, but they cannot form a picture of the whole. This matters most during onboarding, when the product's scope is unfamiliar, and whenever a user's role or responsibilities expand. AI answers questions; structure teaches users which questions exist.

Where AI Fits in Sightline

Jobs are stable and define the product's scope — AI is weakest here because users need to see the full picture to build a mental model. Tasks change periodically and represent the core workflows — AI is moderately effective here for expert users with clear intent, but structure is still needed for browsing and discovery. Activities change frequently and represent the in-context actions — AI is strongest here, resolving the right action within a known context faster than any menu can.

The Practical Division

Primary navigation (Jobs): Stays structural. This is where users learn the product's scope and build spatial memory.

Secondary navigation (Tasks): Hybrid. Structure for browsing and discovery; AI as a shortcut for expert users with clear intent.

In-context navigation (Activities): AI's strongest territory. Contextual actions, smart defaults, and intent resolution outperform static menus at this level.

A well-structured JTBD hierarchy doesn't compete with AI-assisted navigation — it makes it better. The AI needs the same semantic structure to resolve intent correctly. Sightline builds the architecture that AI shortcuts through, not architecture that AI replaces.
